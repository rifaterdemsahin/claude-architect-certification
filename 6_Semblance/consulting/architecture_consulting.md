   What a flywheel truly is. It's a heavy wheel that's brutally hard to start turning, but once it's moving, its own stored momentum makes each push easier, and it keeps spinning on the energy you've already put in. Jim Collins and later Amazon borrowed it for strategy to mean a self-reinforcing loop: each stage feeds and accelerates the next, and the output of the last stage loops back to power the first вАФ so the whole system compounds and gets easier to turn with every rotation.
   The thing that makes it a flywheel and not just a nice metaphor is the loop-back. A funnel is linear: awareness вЖТ interest вЖТ purchase вЖТ done. Each customer falls through once, nothing compounds. A flywheel bends that line into a circle where the end feeds the beginning. The honest test is two questions: does each turn make the next turn easier? and does the output reinvest into the input? If yes, it's a flywheel. If not, it's a funnel with an arrow drawn back on it.
   Now apply that honestly to your case. Your loop вАФ learn вЖТ build вЖТ certify вЖТ publish вЖТ sell вАФ can be a genuine flywheel, because four things compound across cycles: audience, credibility, your body of work, and revenue. That's real compounding.
   But вАФ and this is the truthful part вАФ it's only a flywheel if you deliberately close the loop. If you treat each video and course as a one-off and start cold every time, you've got a funnel, not a flywheel, no matter what the infographic says. The flywheel is a design choice: you have to reinvest each cycle's output (audience, trust, assets, money) into the next cycle. It isn't automatic.
